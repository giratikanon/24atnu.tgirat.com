The roots of Multi-Ethnic InterVarsity

The InterVarsity organization first got its start at the University of Cambridge, England in 1877. British students gathered to study the bible, pray and spread the word of Christianity against the wishes of some university administrators. The name InterVarsity came from the prefix inter, signifying between, and the British word varsity, meaning college level students. By 1941, the InterVarsity Christian Fellowship/USA officially took root in the United States and chapters began springing up on college campuses across the country.

The Northwestern branch of InterVarsity has been in existence for more than 40 years. In that time the organization has gone through an evolution. By 1990, ethnic specific chapters of the Christian organization formed on campus, beginning with the incorporation of Asian American InterVarsity and continuing with an African American chapter and a Hispanic bible study group.

More than a decade later, the original chapter of InterVarsity at Northwestern, InterVarsity Christian Fellowship, began to take note of problems resulting from divisions between people of different ethnicities. It changed its name and refocused its mission to reach out to a larger swath of Christian students. The renamed Multi-Ethnic InterVarsity (MEIV) seeks to encourage interracial unity and the development of ethnic identities.

“So many injustices come from issues of racism and we are really able to talk about it.” says Northwestern’s Multi-Ethnic InterVarsity president, Caroline Na (Comm ’08). “[MEIV] is a place where people can feel comfortable, safe and bring up some of these hard issues.”
