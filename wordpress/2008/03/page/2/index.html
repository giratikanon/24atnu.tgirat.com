Sidebar

Thursday, March 6th, 2008

While the medical school admissions process is enough to panic any pre-med student, those in Northwestern’s Honors Program in Medical Education (HPME) know before they even graduate high school that they will have a spot in medical school. Begun in 1961, HPME grants simultaneous admission into a Northwestern undergraduate college and the Feinberg School of Medicine in downtown Chicago. Students typically leave the Evanston campus after just three years, provided that they keep up their GPA.

“It was funny because you’d be in bio class or chem class and everyone around you would be like ‘Oh, those stupid HPMEs, I hate them so much, they’re going to med school and we’re still struggling,’” Wang said. “But it was nice, it was really low pressure and you’re not stuck in undergrad working just to build up your resume.”

As an economics major, Wang tried to stay away from science classes as much as he could. Instead of doing outreach or volunteer programs, he danced hip-hop in Fusion.

“It was opportunity for me to get out and explore the world before I’d be tied down to something science-related,” Wang said.

The pressure is on now though. Medical school exams are every six weeks, which doesn’t leave much time for slacking off.

“I’ve never worked so hard in my life,” Wang said.

Wang said it would have been nice to share a fourth year with his friends back in Evanston, but that he is glad he’s now on a “bigger path in life.”

Sidebar

Wednesday, March 5th, 2008

It’s perhaps one of the strangest things a university could pride itself on.

Yet every tour group always stops in front of the historic Rock, and the rule is explained: According to tradition, painters of The Rock must guard it 24 hours in advance before it can paint it.

It’s a tradition that students have been abiding by for about 60 years now. Painting it started out as a prank, but today, student groups use it as a way to promote an event or even support a political candidate.

When Students for Ron Paul painted The Rock, it showed obvious support for the Republican presidential candidate. But it also showed the group’s impact on university policies. Student groups were not previously able to use university resources to promote political candidates, such as Ron Paul, because of the university’s tax-exempt status. That was before certain student leaders decided to challenge the policy.

Days after the Associated Student Government passed a bill to urge the body’s president to lobby university administration to re-examine its tax-exempt policy, Students for Ron Paul celebrated by showing support in a way it couldn’t before.

In order to paint The Rock, the group had to guard it. Some had been camping out in front of it for almost half the day, but it didn’t matter.

The group was small, but it was cohesive. Members faced difficulties — they learned the hard way that paint indeed does freeze — but camaraderie (and a car to get another bucket of paint) pulled the group together. And through the night, members joked about the presidential candidates.

Gung-ho, one zealous Ron Paul fan screamed, “It’s Ron Paul time, damn it!”
